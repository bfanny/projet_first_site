The poisonous frogs

Frogs are amphibians that have four legs and are found in very different regions in relation to the climate (temperate, humid, dry, etc.) or natural environment (on land, in water). If certain common species in Europe do not represent no danger to humans, in some countries of the world, frogs have very toxic characteristics.

How does their body harbor poison?

Sequencing the DNA of venomous frog species using epibatidine, Rebecca Tarvin and her colleagues found that frog acetylcholine receptors were slightly deformed; results that the team recently published in the journal Science. From a genetic point of view, this modification had to be subtle. Acetylcholine and epibatidine bind to the same place on the nerve receptor: if the mutations change the form of the receptor too drastically, acetylcholine would not be able to perform its essential daily tasks. Other structural adjustments along the receiver have circumvented the problem. Epibatidine is unable to "recognize" the acetylcholine receptor of venomous frogs; but acetylcholine can, thus saving the frogs from the effects of their toxic infusion. In addition, it seems that frogs have encountered this resistance three times during their evolution, testimony of the usefulness of this mutation. " That's wonderful. There are only a handful of examples like the Rebecca Tarvin study, "says Zoltan Takacs, a herpetologist, toxin expert and explorer for National Geographic. "Whatever you discover, this is a very rare breakthrough in the workings of evolution and neurobiology. "
